Identify Needs

How might we assist reviewers with ApplyGrad?

Through a variety of research methods, we summarized the three needs that reviewers have during the review process and that are not well-addressed by the current system.

Need for Integration

We recommend improving ApplyGrad by fitting it better with the reviewer’s natural workflow, so that they do not need to rely on to external tools for help.

However, one pain point of using external tools is that it lacks the support and customization which ApplyGrad offers. Impacts and Benefits


By addressing reviewers’ need of integration, we can greatly help them in efficiently finishing their work. Integrating features that can be found outside of ApplyGrad will help people pay attention to the task at hand. Integrating data manipulation functions will save a few hours between switching tabs and making reviewers receive their assigned applications faster.

In general, by integrating ApplyGrad to reviewers’ current natural workflow, we can really have a system that assist reviewers’ work to read and synthesize applications instead of hindering it, and greatly improving reviewers’ efficiency with the increasing number of applications they receive each year.

Recommendations


There are many existing features in ApplyGrad that reviewers use to help them get to the next stage of review. However, many of these features are underutilized. In addition, people have gotten familiar with outside tools that for features are not available in ApplyGrad. ApplyGrad’s strength is its availability to be customized and quick support. No matter which design direction we go into, we must account for ApplyGrad’s unique history and add careful features that help get the users into the next stage faster.

  • Make useful functions like ‘semi-blind’ review more clear
  • Understand reviewers’ current workflow and find ways to support them in ApplyGrad
  • Add documentation and informational buttons so that people can understand how to use functions
  • Customize settings that can be shown to each department

Need for Synthesis

Another need for reviewers emerged through our research. Typically, for each applicant, they need to send the following documents for a complete application:

  • Resume
  • Statement of Purpose
  • Transcript
  • 3 Letters of Recommendation
  • GRE Score (optional)
  • TOEFL/IELTS score for non-native speakers


In addition, for some programs, applicants are also asked to submit video essays or portfolios. Different pieces of information about an applicant are spreaded out in different materials. When reviewers are reading these materials, they want a holistic view of the applicant, but lack the support to piece together information across documents. One reviewer specifically mentioned that:

[It would be nice to] have something to track discussion and take notes on what has been discussed about each candidate.


This creates two pain points for reviewers.

Pain Points

1. Reviewers need to remember and combine pieces of information from different materials to form a holistic view of an application.

Given the number of documents for an application, reviewers usually conduct multi-tasking and swivel between documents to get background and context about an applicant. During our conceptual prototype testing, 3 out of 3 reviewers look for recurring subjects in material. Reviewers will make mental notes while looking across materials and look for things that they do not understand.


Interviews with reviewers from MSCB & MSAII

2. Reviewers are frustrated with time-consuming, repetitive tasks, but prioritize properly evaluating applicants regardless of how long it might take.

Even though reviewing one application takes much time and energy, reviewers expressed the desire to thoroughly read application materials and make fair judgement. This creates a conflict of effectiveness and efficiency, as a reviewer puts it:

“..tension between wanting to be thorough and holistic and wanting to save time to jump to important things.”


Below is a screenshot from our conceptual prototype, where we prepared fake applicant data and observed users work in their own computer environment. It’s time consuming switching between screens and many reviewers will often make reviews in split-screen mode. This allows reviewers to make comments as they go along the review easily.



Impacts and Benefits


Addressing this need can significantly improve reviewers’ efficiency as reading application documents is one of the most important things they do in their review work. Some of the benefits include:

  • Reducing the cognitive load for reviewers can lead to better, more efficient, and more thorough review of applicants.
  • Helping reviewers remember details of applications can facilitate more productive and nuanced discussions during admission committee meetings.
  • Introducing collaboration opportunities between reviewers working on the same application during review can eliminate time-consuming manual tasks.


Recommendations


So how do we move forward on this need? Potential design solutions for integrating a system for reviewers to track and share data about applicants across documents and between committees include:

  • An annotation system for reviewers to mark up documents (highlight, comment, link between documents) during review
  • Modular note-taking system that can be pinned across screens and documents
  • Machine-learning highlighting system that can highlight factual information across documents

Need for Fairness

It’s of great importance for reviewers to conduct their review work fairly and free from their personal biases. Reviewers put this as one of their priorities in work, however, most current efforts to promote fairness are not supported by ApplyGrad, but are done manually by admission administrators or directors.

The biggest challenge in the whole system is making sure there is consistency in the judgement.


Pain Points

Given that fairness is a very broad concept, there are a lot of aspects in review that admission committees are trying to ensure fairness in. Through our research, we identified two major pain points for reviewers to address fairness concerns.

1. Reviewers want to make fair evaluation calibration but it’s not supported by ApplyGrad

Currently, reviewers seize to achieve a good calibration in their evaluations of application materials, including transcripts, resumes, and recommendation letters. Well-calibrated means that reviewers have a good understanding of the application pool and give consistent evaluation across different applications. Some reviewers take a look at a few applications to understand the application pool before doing actual evaluation and decision making, to form a basis for them to calibrate. There’s no way in ApplyGrad to check if reviewers are well-calibrated in their evaluations.

  • Lenient reviewers might score applicants higher than rigid reviewers in general, but ApplyGrad cannot help reviewers or administrators check that.
  • ApplyGrad doesn’t provide information on schools’ grading systems, recommendation letter writers’ writing skills, etc.


“There is no visual layout to tell me how I scored everybody and whether it makes sense.”
–– Reviewer



2. ApplyGrad does not encompass current efforts to ensure fairness, so committees have to come up with their own practices

Current efforts to promote fairness and equity include:

  • Committee meetings at the beginning of each application year to align expectations and metrics reviewers would use.
  • Administrators checking reviewers’ progress and statistics, and nudge them if they identify indicators for bias.
  • Bias Buster workshop sessions done by Dr. Geoff Kaufman for many committees.


Committees of different programs usually have distinct concerns for fairness, which are not yet encompassed by ApplyGrad.

“I’ve been tracking reviewers’ review status and see if they have been spending a decent amount of time on all applicants.”
–– Committee Director


Impacts and Benefits


Ensuring fairness with ApplyGrad can improve the general decision making quality and reduce bias in admission review. Integrating fairness-ensuring features in ApplyGrad can save current manual labor by administrators and reviewers.

Using ApplyGrad to help reviewers ensure fairness can help SCS manage standards to promote diversity, equity, and fairness in admission on an administrative level.

Recommendations


Many opportunities lie in designing ApplyGrad to promote fairness and reduce bias in the review process. Since current efforts have been implemented by individual programs, we can identify current practices that can be assisted or amplified with ApplyGrad.

ApplyGrad can facilitate and introduce efforts to make admission at SCS more fair and equitable by:

  • Introducing features to integrate existing fairness practices into ApplyGrad (e.g. tracking statistics on gender and ethnicity diversity of the admitted group).
  • Providing tools to help reviewers calibrate their reviews and make fair decisions.
  • Reminding reviewers with information about potential biases in ApplyGrad for each step of review and nudge them to take best practices.
  • Aligning caseload assignments with the reviewer’s background and area of expertise.
  • Including customizability to cater to different committees’ distinct needs and practices for fairness.
